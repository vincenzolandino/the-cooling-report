142 Data Center Protests Hit 42 States in One Day. Every Fight Traces Back to a Cooling Tower.

Data center opponents staged 142 protests across 42 states on Saturday, July 18, according to Reuters' coverage of the coordinated push. HumansFirst, a grassroots network co-founded by former Tea Party organizer Amy Kremer, tracked the count. Texas hosted 18 events. Georgia had 11. California logged eight, and Pennsylvania, Florida and Indiana each ran seven, a spread that crosses red counties and blue ones carrying the same complaint. Nobody asked first.

The Water Number That Started It

Ivan DelSol, 54, organized against a proposed data center in Imperial County, California, that would draw 260 million gallons a year from the Colorado River. "It's dystopian that you would use this much fresh water for AI," he told Reuters. That volume is roughly what a mid-size evaporative cooling plant burns through as makeup water in a single year, water lost to the atmosphere the moment a facility picks wet cooling towers over a closed loop or air. The same river is already stretched thin near Phoenix, where data center water use is set to climb ninefold against a supply with no reserve left.

The Complaint Reads Like a Spec Sheet

Kremer framed the backlash as ordinary people discovering a facility was coming to their town with no say in the matter, comparing the mood to the early Tea Party organizing she once led. Eva Cardona, 31, running her first protest in Tyler, Texas, pointed to the same root cause: infrastructure scaling faster than any local review could track. Josh Levi, president of the Data Center Coalition, responded by pointing to ongoing engagement with regulators and residents, the standard industry answer to a complaint that keeps showing up anyway, one that echoes the pattern behind opposition groups doubling to 833 in a single quarter. A June Reuters/Ipsos poll puts a number on the mood: 33% of Americans approve of the current pace of data center construction, and just 14% would welcome one in their own community.

Cooling Choice Is Now a Permitting Variable

This is the part the industry keeps underselling. Water, power bills and pollution are the three complaints named across all 142 events, and each one traces back to a cooling decision engineers make at design time, long before permitting turns political. Evaporative towers pull the water headlines. Air-cooled and closed-loop systems cost more to build and need more floor space, but they skip the aquifer and river fights entirely. North Carolina's House already voted 69 to 44 to require that shift for the largest sites, a preview of the fight now playing out at protest sites nationwide, per the state's push to ban evaporative cooling at hyperscale sites.

Saturday's 142 events read as an opening lap. Kremer is already calling data centers a defining issue for the November midterms, and the state list already maps onto the current hyperscale build zones: Texas, Georgia, the Carolinas, the Sun Belt corridor chasing cheap power and cheap land. Every operator with a permit application still pending should treat the cooling method on that filing as a public argument now, contested in town halls before a single rack ships. Specify wet cooling in a water-stressed county and the protest sign writes itself before the concrete cures.
