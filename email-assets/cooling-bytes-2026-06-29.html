Heatwaves Spike Cooling Demand When the Grid Has Least to Give, and Frontier Data Centers Are Most Exposed.

Cooling already runs about 40 percent of a data center's energy at normal temperatures, and that share climbs during a heatwave exactly when grid power is scarcest. The physics is plain. Hotter outside air means less free cooling, harder-working chillers, and a draw curve that bends up just as utilities ration supply. A study cited by CNBC finds 79 percent of global capacity sits exposed to acute climate hazards, and just over half of it carries chronic heat and drought stress.

79% Capacity exposed to acute climate hazards
~4% Cooling cost cut per 1 °C setpoint rise
$3.3T Added running cost by 2055

The build is moving toward the weather. CNBC reports 64 percent of capacity under construction this year sits outside traditional hubs, pushing into West Texas, Tennessee, Wisconsin, and Ohio, where exposed roof HVAC and cooling towers face tornadoes, hail, and high winds. Severe weather is now the leading cause of loss in Zurich's U.S. builders' risk portfolio. The move for planners is toward warm-water loops, higher chiller setpoints, and heat rejection hardened to survive a storm.

Omen AI Raises $31 Million to Read Coolant Chemistry and Stop the Bacterial Fouling That Shuts Loops Down

Direct-to-chip cooling moves heat with a fluid pressed against the die, and operators raise the water content to absorb more heat per pass. Higher water feeds bacteria, the bacteria foul the loop, and a fouled loop drops the system for five or six hours at a cost Omen puts in the millions per incident. Nava Ventures led the round, with CRV participating, taking total funding to $40 million since the 2024 founding.

5–6 hrs Downtime per fouling incident
$40M Total raised since 2024
Real time Spectrometer reads on the loop

Omen deploys a spectrometer that reads coolant chemistry on the loop and flags bacterial growth early, sold as a fluid-health layer on top of liquid cooling. Caterpillar, Bridgestone, GM, Johnson Controls, and Mann+Hummel sit among named customers, giving Omen a fouling dataset wider than any single hyperscaler. As rack density forces liquid cooling from option to requirement, the loop becomes uptime infrastructure that needs its own telemetry. Procurement teams specifying CDUs now weigh a fluid-monitoring line against five or six hours of dark racks.

GE Vernova Turbines Are Sold Out to 2029, and That Backlog Now Gates When Liquid-Cooled AI Campuses Come Online.

Power is the binding constraint on the AI buildout, and the company that makes the turbines now sets the schedule. GE Vernova's order book is sold out through 2029, with new orders pushing delivery into 2031, a backlog that exceeds 110 GW. Turbine pricing has climbed roughly 300 percent in three years. The company booked $2.4 billion of data-center electrification orders in the first quarter of 2026, more than the entire prior year, with Meta, Google, OpenAI, and Anthropic reportedly lined up for slots.

"A turbine slot is a heat-rejection commitment in disguise. The megawatts a campus secures define the watts it must move off the racks, which is why the turbine queue and the cooling queue rise in lockstep."

The Cooling Report  ·  June 29, 2026

Chevron made it concrete. It ordered seven large GE Vernova turbines for Project Kilby, a 2.67 GW off-grid gas plant near Pecos in West Texas that runs independent of the ERCOT grid and targets first power in 2028. It feeds a Microsoft AI data center under a 20-year agreement. Microsoft committed to a closed-loop cooling system that it says uses less water annually than a typical fast-food restaurant.

Here is the cooling implication. Sold-out turbines through 2029 set the date a high-density campus can energize, which puts CDU procurement, cold-plate orders, and heat-rejection capex downstream of a turbine slot. The thermal design can be finished and the racks can be ready, and none of it matters until the power lands. For cooling vendors, the turbine backlog is the real forward order book, and it reads sold out to the end of the decade.

South Korea Commits 18.4 Gigawatts of AI Data Centers and Pledges 1 Million Tons of Water a Day to Cool Them

18.4 Gigawatts AI data center target by 2035, from 8.4 GW in phase one
1M Tons of water / day Industrial water pledged to the target region
$1.2T Investment drive Over two-thirds of the country's GDP across chips and AI

South Korea unveiled an investment drive of nearly $1.2 trillion across semiconductors, AI data centers, and physical AI. The data center track targets 8.4 gigawatts in an initial phase, scaling to 18.4 by 2035, with a separate 10-gigawatt facility planned by the same year. SK Group, GS Group, and Naver are committing 550 trillion won. A fleet this large running modern accelerators lands well past the point where air alone moves the heat, which puts cold plates, CDUs, and warm-water loops at the center of the build.

The water pledge is the tell. The government promised roughly 1 million tons a day in the target region after concerns about heavy demand from the combined chip and data center cluster. Spread across 18.4 gigawatts of IT load plus four new fabs, that headroom shrinks fast. Evaporative towers buy power efficiency and burn water. Closed-loop and warm-water designs protect the watershed and shift the penalty back onto the grid. Every operator in Honam picks a side of that ledger before pouring a foundation, and the pick is a cooling decision first.
