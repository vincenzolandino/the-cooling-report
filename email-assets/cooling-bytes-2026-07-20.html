Four stories on the same question: who checks the number. Hut 8 Signs a Second $9.8 Billion Lease, Doubling One Tenant to 704 Megawatts. The Water Number Is Why the Deal Could Close This Fast.

Hut 8 signed a second 15-year lease worth $9.8 billion at its Beacon Point campus in Nueces County, Texas, the same undisclosed, high investment-grade tenant doubling its footprint to 704 megawatts. The campus's base-term contract value now stands at $19.6 billion, with renewal options that could push it to $50.2 billion. None of it survives without the thermal engineering underneath.

$19.6B Contracted campus value after the second lease
704 MW One tenant's total footprint at Beacon Point
57% Density increase engineered into the same acreage

Cooling at Beacon Point runs on a closed loop filled once, roughly 1.8 million gallons trucked in from outside Nueces County, then recirculated for four to seven years before it needs a refill. Hailey Miller, Hut 8's senior director of regulatory and government affairs, put it plainly: "Under no circumstance will we use municipal water for cooling." Elsewhere in the market, neocloud deals keep stalling because colocation providers won't sign without investment-grade credit behind the lease. Twice now, at the same site, the market has answered yes.

PJM's Own Market Monitor Says Data Centers Cost $6.3 Billion in the Latest Auction. He Wants Them Out of the Shared Market.

Joseph Bowring, PJM's independent market monitor, attributes $6.3 billion of the grid operator's latest $16.4 billion capacity auction to data center load, and $29.4 billion across the last four auctions combined. His proposal: move hyperscalers into a dedicated capacity market where they procure their own generation instead of bidding against homes and factories for the same megawatts.

$29.4B Data center share across four PJM auctions
$16.4B Total cost of the latest auction
1 Market monitor pushing to split the market in two

A capacity auction prices reserve margin, and reserve margin is set by the worst thermal day on the grid, the afternoon every chiller and CRAH unit pulls max power at once. That is the same mechanic behind the White House's ratepayer pledge last week: cooling-driven peak demand is what utilities size feeders and substations against, and what Bowring wants hyperscalers to price for themselves. A dedicated capacity market would not eliminate the load. It would just put the cooling bill where it belongs, on the account that created it.

Sierra Club, NRDC, and Earthjustice Just Nationalized the Data Center Fight. It Still Comes Down to a Water Permit and a Chiller Complaint.

Sierra Club, NRDC, Earthjustice, and Food and Water Watch are building a national coordination network for data center opposition, according to Politico's reporting on the shift. Eighteen months of federal losses pushed the strategy: no moratorium bill has cleared Congress, no federal permitting rule has slowed a single project, and national environmental groups spent that stretch losing on the issue that most animates their local chapters.

"National coalitions and reelection strategy make the headline. A water permit for a cooling tower and a noise variance for a chiller plant still make the case."

The Cooling Report  ·  July 20, 2026

The funding and staff time are new. The target is not. Sanders and AOC's federal moratorium bill went nowhere in Congress, and eleven states are weighing construction pauses of their own with no federal help behind them. What is actually winning cases at the local level is the same mechanic activist coalitions have coordinated on for over a year: water withdrawal permits for evaporative cooling towers, and noise variances for chiller plants and diesel generators running next to a subdivision.

A national press operation can put a Sierra Club spokesperson on cable news. It cannot make a planning board approve a water permit it was already going to deny. The nationalized network's real value is speeding up a process that already works: finding the cooling architecture decision hiding inside every permit application, and putting a lawyer on it before the hearing date instead of after.

Gary Pinkston Told Kansas Radio He Was the Master Developer. Four Days Later, He Swore Under Oath He Has No Interest in the Project.

4 Days Between the radio interview and the sworn testimony
1 GW Campus Size Flint Hills Digital Campus, proposed for Emporia, Kansas
0 Cooling Method Disclosed Still undecided, according to the Kansas Reflector

Gary Pinkston told Kansas radio listeners he was the master developer behind Emporia's proposed 1-gigawatt Flint Hills Digital Campus, according to the Kansas Reflector's reporting. Four days later, in a sworn filing, he said he has no interest in the project at all. Residents at the hearing called the reversal exactly what it looked like: a credibility problem dressed up as a legal technicality.

Nobody in Emporia has been told what the campus will actually cool with. A gigawatt of AI compute pulls a specific thermal load regardless of who signs the permit, and the county cannot size a water allocation or a noise ordinance against a cooling architecture the developer will not name. Pinkston's testimony answers a legal question about liability. It does not answer the one the town actually asked: what pulls the heat off a gigawatt of silicon, and where does the water for it come from.
