NVIDIA's New Data Center Design Cuts Onsite Water Use. The AI Water Problem Moves Upstream to the Power Plant.

NVIDIA published a reference design for liquid-cooled AI factories built on its DSX architecture, and the headline number is a water figure. The Rubin-generation design runs 100 percent liquid cooling with coolant entering cold plates near 45 degrees Celsius and leaving around 55, hot enough for dry coolers to reject the heat without mechanical chillers in many climates. The closed loop recirculates a mix of roughly 75 percent water and 25 percent propylene glycol and consumes no fresh water once filled.

2.6M → 0 Facility cooling water, gal/MW/yr
45 °C Coolant supply, chillers eliminated
$4M+ Annual savings at 50 MW

The onsite cut is real. Most of AI's water footprint was never onsite. Thermoelectric power plants consume water for their own cooling, roughly 1.17 liters per kilowatt-hour for gas and 2.2 for coal against 0.01 for wind, and fossil generation still supplies about half of data center power. Closing the loop in the facility yard reassigns the water debt to whoever generates the electricity, which puts the onsite portion NVIDIA addresses at roughly a quarter to a third of the whole.

3D-Printed Copper Cold Plates Cut Data Center Cooling Energy by Up to 97 Percent in Lab Tests

A University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign team, working with the San Diego firm Fabric8Labs, used topology optimization to design copper cold plates with jagged internal channels no machining or brazing could reproduce, then built them with electrochemical additive manufacturing at 30-to-50-micrometer precision. Published in Cell Reports Physical Science in May 2026, the work argues the channel geometry, not the coolant, is where the next efficiency gains are hiding.

32% / 68% Better cooling / less pressure drop
550 → 11 MW Cooling draw on a 1 GW site
1.011 Projected PUE, optimized loop

The baseline is the load-bearing detail. The 97 percent headline is a facility-scale model measured against conventional air cooling, not against a modern liquid deployment, so it flatters the plate the way any liquid-versus-air figure does. The component-level gains, 32 percent better cooling and 68 percent less pressure drop against a conventional plate, are the real lab measurements. The open question is whether the electrochemical process can hit hyperscale volumes and unit costs.

The World Economic Forum Is Taking Orbital Data Centers Seriously as a Cooling Play. Physics Still Sets the Terms.

The case for putting data centers in orbit has always been a cooling and resource argument before it was a compute argument. Above the atmosphere there is continuous solar power, no rezoning fight, no aquifer to draw down, and a heat sink that costs nothing to reach in the 2.7-kelvin background of deep space. When an institution like the WEF treats orbital compute as a serious answer to terrestrial water and power constraints, it is signaling how tight those constraints have become on the ground.

"Operators do not seriously price out launching racks to space because it is convenient. They do it because terrestrial water and power constraints have tightened to the point where a 1,600-square-meter radiator in vacuum starts to pencil against a water permit that will never be granted."

The Cooling Report  ·  June 23, 2026

The physics counterweight is unforgiving. A two-sided radiator near 20 degrees Celsius emits only about 633 watts per square meter, so a one-megawatt orbital facility needs on the order of 1,600 square meters of radiator surface, roughly four tennis courts per megawatt. Running hotter cuts the area by about half and pushes the silicon toward its thermal ceiling. Surface area is mass, panels run 5 to 9 kilograms per square meter, and mass is launch cost.

The concept is past slideware. Starcloud put an NVIDIA H100 in orbit in late 2025, Axiom Space began deploying orbital nodes in January 2026, and SpaceX has shown a compute satellite with a deployable liquid radiator. Maintenance and latency remain unsolved. The honest reading is that physics still sets the terms in orbit, and the rising seriousness of the conversation is a barometer of the limits closing in at home.

China Built the World's Fastest Supercomputer, and It Is Aimed at Science Over AI

2.198 Exaflops, HPL LineShine takes TOP500 number one from El Capitan
42.2 Megawatts Power draw, nearly all of it heat the plant must reject
13.79M CPU cores All-CPU design, no NVIDIA or AMD accelerators on board

LineShine, installed at the National Supercomputing Centre in Shenzhen, is the first system to sustain more than two exaflops of double-precision performance on CPUs alone, ending El Capitan's run at the top. It carries no advanced AI accelerators, likely because the tools to make those chips remain under US export controls, and on the mixed-precision benchmark that resembles AI work it places fourth. Fastest in the world and fastest for AI have become two different titles.

For the cooling industry the distinction shapes the room. Nearly all of that 42.2 megawatts converts to heat at extreme density, and a top-of-TOP500 machine implies a cooling plant operating at a correspondingly advanced level. China has been pushing hard on cold plates, immersion, and materials like diamond-copper coatings, because every flop on the score sheet is a watt a heat-rejection system had to carry first.
