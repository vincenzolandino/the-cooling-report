xAI Pulled 812,502 Gallons from Memphis's Drinking Water Aquifer in a Single Day

The Memphis Sand Aquifer supplies drinking water to roughly one million people across the greater Memphis area. On its highest single draw day on record, xAI's Colossus facility pulled 812,502 gallons from that same aquifer.

In March 2026 alone, xAI drew more than 25 million gallons total. The facility runs 50 portable methane-fired generators to power its compute load while permanent grid infrastructure catches up. Memphis Light, Gas, and Water CEO Doug McGowen extended a rate of 19 cents per 100 gallons. Standard Memphis customers pay 32.

25M gal March 2026 total draw
50 Methane generators on-site
$120M Wastewater treatment overrun
−40% Water rate vs. standard customers

The wastewater treatment facility built to handle Colossus's discharge was budgeted at $80 million. The final cost was $200 million. That gap reflects what happens when thermal infrastructure is scoped before the full compute load is specified. The $120 million overrun suggests the facility was designed for a substantially lower daily water demand than it is now handling.

Phoenix Data Center Water Use Is About to Jump Ninefold. The Colorado River Has No Reserve Left.

Phoenix-area data center water consumption is projected to climb from 385 million gallons per year to 3.7 billion gallons per year, a ninefold increase, as AI infrastructure accelerates across the Sonoran Desert. Lake Mead is approximately one-third full. Lake Powell sits below one-quarter capacity. Arizona has already absorbed mandatory Colorado River allocation cuts. The basin has no surplus allocation on record.

The regional picture is worse than Phoenix alone. A study covering Arizona, Colorado, Nevada, New Mexico, and Utah projects combined data center water use reaching 7 billion gallons per year by 2035. Nevada data centers may need nearly three times the output of Hoover Dam in additional generation capacity to support the state's pipeline.

385M → 3.7B Gallons/yr, Phoenix projection
7B gal 5-state total by 2035
⅓ Lake Mead capacity remaining

Phoenix suburbs have adopted industrial water caps. Marana, Arizona has barred data centers from using potable water entirely. Tucson tied permits to zero-water pledges as a condition of approval.

Opposition to Data Center Permits Is Now Coordinated Across Causes. The Funding Question Is Secondary.

Opposition movements previously organized around single issues, water rights, local zoning, or environmental review, are now coordinating across those issues and arriving at permit hearings together. An operator that prepared a strong water use case may face organized opposition on noise, grid strain, and community character simultaneously, from groups that share research, legal strategy, and media relationships. That coordination compresses the time available to respond and multiplies the number of issues requiring public defense.

"Commission approvals that were administrative three years ago are now political. County commissioners who vote yes on a data center project are now approving something that may end up on a referendum ballot."

The Cooling Report  ·  May 25, 2026

Fox News and named experts allege China-linked funding in data center opposition networks. The coordination itself is documentable in permit records, separate from the funding question. The Box Elder County referendum effort is real. Millville, New Jersey passed a data center ban. New York had an active moratorium push. Ohio municipalities have passed moratoriums.

The $64 billion in projects blocked or delayed by community opposition is a figure drawn from project filings. The pattern is visible across jurisdictions, across political environments, and across project types.

For operators, the source of opposition is secondary to what it costs. A 100 MW data center under construction but unable to commission because of a permit challenge costs an estimated $50,000 to $100,000 per day in financing costs (estimate, based on typical capital structures at that scale). A six-month delay on a $500 million build is a material impact on project returns. Some lenders and insurers are beginning to treat permit risk as a pricing factor. This is based on developer conversations, not a confirmed market-wide shift.

Developers that engage communities before the permit application, rather than after the opposition has organized, continue to have better outcomes.

New York Is Weighing Who Pays for the Grid Upgrades Data Centers Require. The Answer Changes Everything.

500 Megawatts Single facility, Genesee County NY
$1.4B Tax breaks For that same facility
≈NYC Total pipeline load Proposed AI data centers across New York may approach New York City's entire annual electricity consumption

New York state regulators are weighing rules that would require large energy users to fund a greater share of the grid infrastructure their power demands require. If those rules take effect, the economics of data centers in the New York development pipeline change materially. Developers who modeled their projects on current cost-allocation assumptions built in a subsidy that may not survive the regulatory cycle.

Governor Hochul opposes a moratorium. She has not blocked the cost-allocation review. That distinction matters: a state that stays open to data centers but forces them to internalize grid upgrade costs is making a fundamentally different offer than one that socializes those costs across all ratepayers. Every project in the New York pipeline should be stress-tested against both scenarios.
