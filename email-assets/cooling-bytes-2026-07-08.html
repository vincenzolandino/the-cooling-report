TVA Bets Its Grid on 1,340 Data Centers Worth $912 Billion, and Builds a Rate Class Around Never Turning Them Off.

The Tennessee Valley Authority's 2026 resource plan counts roughly 1,340 planned data centers across its seven-state footprint. Its 2024 plan never mentioned them. Demand could climb 60 percent by 2040, and TVA is evaluating up to 26 gigawatts of new gas while pushing coal retirements at Cumberland City and Kingston out to 2039.

1,340 Planned data centers in the IRP
26 GW New gas generation under review
2039 Pushed-back coal retirement year

TVA is also building a dedicated rate class for data centers, with a capacity commitment charge paid upfront, before a facility ever draws power. The Tennessee Valley has surface water most Western sites can't count on, so the binding constraint isn't gallons. It's megawatts, and TVA is now pricing every one of them whether the cooling plant wastes it or not.

DOE Closes $3.26 Billion to Rewire Texas for 41 Gigawatts of Data Center Load

The Department of Energy closed a $3.26 billion loan to AEP Texas on July 8, funding roughly 100 transmission projects and 2,800 miles of reconductored line. AEP has already signed agreements backing up to 41 gigawatts of new load through 2030, most of it data centers pushing ERCOT's interconnection queue past hundreds of gigawatts.

100 Transmission projects funded
2,800 mi Line reconductored or rebuilt
41 GW Signed load through 2030

Grid money buys wire capacity. It does not buy the CDUs, chillers, and cold plates that decide whether those campuses actually energize on schedule. A reconductored line reaching a substation on time puts zero racks online by itself. AEP's 41 gigawatts of signed load will only energize as fast as the cooling procurement behind it clears, and that supply chain, not the transmission backlog, is setting the real go-live date.

Oregon Raises Data Center Rates 29.7 Percent and Cuts Everyone Else's Bill.

Oregon regulators approved a 29.7 percent electricity rate increase for data centers in Portland General Electric's territory, effective this week, while cutting residential rates 1.3 percent in the same order. The steepest bill lands on the Hillsboro colocation cluster: Flexential, Stack Infrastructure, QTS, NTT, and Digital Realty. The increase runs through House Bill 3546, the state's POWER Act, which forces large power users to fund the infrastructure their own growth demands.

"A rate class built on contracted capacity does not know the difference between a wasted watt and a working one. It charges for both the same way."

The Cooling Report  ·  July 8, 2026

Oregon's new Schedule 96 rate class pins minimum demand charges to 90 percent of a facility's contracted capacity, whether that capacity gets used or not. Loads above 100 megawatts pay an added penny per kilowatt-hour, routed into low-income energy assistance. PGE's data center draw grew sixfold in five years, from 50 average megawatts in 2020 to more than 300 by mid-2025.

A facility running PUE 1.5 instead of 1.2 now pays that 29.7 percent markup on the wasted third of its power bill, every month, for as long as Schedule 96 stands. Retrofitting to direct-to-chip cooling or replacing an aging chiller plant stops being a sustainability slide and becomes a number finance can track against the new rate class, line by line.

Eleven States Are Weighing a Pause on New Data Centers, and New York's Freeze Bill Is Sitting on Hochul's Desk

11 States Weighing legislation to pause new construction
10 Days Hochul's window to sign, veto, or amend New York's bill
$1.6B Lost in 2025 Virginia tax revenue given up to data center incentives

New York's Responsible Data Center Development Act cleared both chambers in June and now sits on Governor Hochul's desk. Maine's governor already vetoed a similar 18-month moratorium in April, and the House fell short of an override, 72 to 65. Moody's flagged the infrastructure cost strain as a credit risk if cities can't recover it from the data centers themselves, though bond analysts stopped short of calling it a crisis.

A one-year freeze does not touch a single running facility. It touches the next one in line, and every CDU, chiller, and cooling tower ordered against a permit that hasn't been filed yet goes on hold with it. Utilities financing grid buildouts against projected data center load are betting that thermal demand lands on the old schedule. Eleven pending bills say otherwise.
