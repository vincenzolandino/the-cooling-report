Meta Is Doubling Its Louisiana Data Center to 5 Gigawatts and $50 Billion. Its Own Water Permit Allows 14 Times What It Says It Needs.

Meta will more than double its Hyperion campus in Richland Parish, Louisiana, raising total investment from $10 billion to as much as $50 billion and pushing the site toward 5 gigawatts of capacity, according to NOLA.com's coverage of the expansion. The state granted Meta a water permit covering up to 23 million gallons a day. Meta's own projection for the finished facility lands at 500 to 600 million gallons a year, roughly 1.5 million gallons a day. The permit runs close to 14 times the company's own number.

$50B Total investment, up from $10 billion
5 GW Compute capacity Meta is building toward
10 Gas plants Entergy has proposed to power the site

Meta describes the cooling system as closed loop, with makeup water needed mainly in the hottest stretches of the year. Entergy Louisiana has proposed seven new gas-fired plants on top of three already under construction, adding 7,400 megawatts of gas alongside a 2,500-megawatt request for solar and wind. Logan Burke of the Alliance for Affordable Energy calls the buildout overwhelming: three plants tripled to ten in under a year. The real test lands after 2028, when Meta's actual water draw either tracks its own estimate or climbs toward the permitted ceiling as rack density rises.

JD Vance Is Renting a Virginia Farm From a Data Center Land Baron Who Sold Acreage Down the Road for $615 Million

Vice President JD Vance is leasing part of a nearly 500-acre farm outside Middleburg, Virginia, from a firm controlled by Chuck Kuhn, founder of JK Moving Services and Loudoun County's largest private landowner, eight months after Kuhn sold 97 acres of nearby data center land for $615 million, according to Daily Kos's coverage. Vance's attorney says the vice president will pay market rate. Nobody has produced evidence of a quid pro quo.

$57M → $615M What Kuhn's Twin Creeks parcel sold for, then resold for
5 Two-story data centers approved on the 97-acre site
12-18 mo Lead time on chillers, CDUs, and switchgear, permit or not

The parcel carries its own dedicated Dominion Energy substation, the detail that actually moved the price. A two-story data center is a structural decision before it is a cooling decision, and running chilled water risers vertically instead of across one slab changes pump head, floor loading, and redundancy for every loop above grade. Trump's July 2025 executive order accelerating federal permitting can compress a timeline to weeks. It cannot compress the order book for chillers and switchgear, which still runs 12 to 18 months no matter who signs the approval.

96 Pennsylvania Landowners Split $586 Million Near a Nuclear Plant. Regulators Just Cut Amazon's Water Request by More Than Half.

Ninety-six families in Salem Township, Pennsylvania sold roughly 1,700 acres of farmland to data-center developer QTS for $586 million, an average of $330,000 an acre, according to The Wall Street Journal's coverage of the deal. Marilee and David Kiliti's 89-acre farm alone sold for more than $22 million. QTS is owned by Blackstone. Its assemblage sits west of Talen's Susquehanna nuclear plant, a 2.5-gigawatt facility that gave this stretch of Luzerne County its value long before AI arrived.

"Same river. Same plant. Two developers, two cooling architectures, and one regulator that already decided how much of the Susquehanna leaves the watershed."

The Cooling Report  ·  July 13, 2026

QTS says its buildings run on a closed-loop system and will not consume river water once operational. Amazon Data Services, building an adjacent campus off the same nuclear plant, petitioned to raise its consumptive-use limit from 60,000 gallons a day to 277,000. The Susquehanna River Basin Commission ruled in March, granting 129,000 gallons a day: more than double the old ceiling, and less than half of what Amazon asked for.

A second assemblage is already moving nearby: 200 landowners across more than 4,000 acres, eyeing a combined $1.3 billion. Salem Township's land brokers priced the power a nuclear plant guarantees. The next assemblage is about to find out what the commission prices the water at.

Denton Is Organizing Against Two Data Centers Finishing Construction This Fall. Only One Has Disclosed How It Cools Itself.

8.5 DFW Airports Equivalent grid draw of both projects combined
82,000 Homes Power draw versus Denton's 57,000 total households
0 gal Disclosed by QumulusAI Core Scientific has not said publicly which method it uses

Denton, Texas residents get their pitch this Thursday: organize against two AI data centers finishing construction this fall, according to KERA News's coverage. DSA Denton and Revolutionary Front are hosting the town hall. Core Scientific's 78-acre, 260-megawatt campus runs direct-to-chip liquid loops. QumulusAI's 4-acre, 20-megawatt site runs a closed loop of non-conductive fluid that its director of engineering, Spencer Smith, says needs zero water at all, chasing a PUE of 1.1.

The water argument only half applies. QumulusAI has already put its answer on the record. Core Scientific has not said publicly which method it uses, whether dry coolers, a closed fluid loop, or the kind of open evaporative towers that draw from the same municipal supply residents are worried about. Every one of the 86 North Texas projects still in the pipeline faces the same choice, and the cooling architecture decision is the one that actually decides whether the next fight is about water.
