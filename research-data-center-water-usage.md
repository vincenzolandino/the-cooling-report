## 3. WHY DATA CENTERS USE SO MUCH WATER / COOLING TECHNIQUES

### Why Water?
Water absorbs and carries heat efficiently, making it ideal for removing the massive heat generated by servers and networking equipment. Cooling accounts for up to 20% of a data center's total energy consumption and, if water-based, up to 95% of its water consumption.

### Cooling Techniques:

**Evaporative Cooling (Cooling Towers)**
- Uses the same principle as perspiration: water evaporates over fill/coils to reject heat, discharging warm air to the atmosphere.
- Extremely energy efficient but consumes large volumes of water, especially in hot, dry climates.
- Typical design allows evaporation estimated at 1% of cooling tower water flow rate, plus 0.5% blow-down losses.
- WUE can be as high as 2.5 L/kWh (approximately 660 gallons per MWh at that rate).
- Traditional evaporative cooling can consume up to 1.5 million liters of water per day per hyperscale data center.

**Adiabatic Cooling**
- Hybrid coolers combining evaporative cooling and dry cooling.
- Uses latent heat transfer from water evaporation to pre-cool entering air.
- Operates as a dry cooler whenever possible (lower temps, lower loads), dramatically reducing water consumption.
- Only activates water evaporation during peak ambient temperatures or high loads.

**Direct-to-Chip (Liquid) Cooling**
- Circulates liquid directly on CPU or GPU, then passes through a heat exchanger to cool it before recirculation.
- Significantly reduces water consumption compared to evaporative systems.
- Can cut cooling energy use by up to 60%.
- Microsoft's new design avoids 125+ million liters of water per year per data center.

**Immersion Cooling**
- Entire servers submerged in a dielectric fluid.
- Approaches zero water consumption.
- 2-phase immersion cooling maintains primary loop fluid temperatures of 53C or higher, enabling dry coolers to reject heat efficiently.
- PUE of 1.02-1.03 even in the warmest climates.
- Cooling energy reductions of more than 90% in high-density AI environments.
- Most sustainable but also most expensive option.

### Actual Gallon Numbers:
- **Medium-sized data center:** ~110 million gallons/year (~301,000 gallons/day)
- **Large data center:** Up to 5 million gallons/day (~1.8 billion gallons/year)
- **Typical data center:** 300,000 gallons/day (equivalent to ~1,000 households)
- **Large AI data center:** Up to 5 million gallons/day (equivalent to a town of 50,000 residents)
- **U.S. data centers collectively (2021):** 449 million gallons/day; 163.7 billion gallons/year
- **Northern Virginia data centers (2023):** ~2 billion gallons; 63% increase from 2019

## 4. REDDIT / PUBLIC CONCERNS ABOUT DATA CENTER WATER USAGE

### Main Concerns Raised:

**Community Water Supply Competition**
- In Newton County, Georgia, Meta's data center was permitted to use over 500,000 gallons of water daily. Residents reported lower water pressure and supply issues.
- A Microsoft data center cluster in Iowa consumed 6% of the local water district's monthly supply while training GPT-4.
- Large operators (Alibaba, Alphabet, Amazon, Digital Realty, Meta, Microsoft, Tencent) have all faced allegations involving community opposition and scrutiny over water and power use (as of November 2025).

**Lack of Transparency**
- Companies rarely disclose exactly how much water their data centers use.
- Microsoft provides only global operations data, not data center-specific breakdowns.
- Researchers and journalists have struggled to obtain facility-level data.

**Water Stress Concerns**
- Many data centers are located in already water-stressed regions.
- Chile: Nearing 15th consecutive year of unprecedented drought with more than a dozen data centers constructed/planned near Santiago.
- Southern Nevada banned evaporative cooling in all new developments due to high water stress.

**Policy Responses (2024-2025)**
- California introduced SB 58 (January 2025): tax credit for data centers adopting sustainable practices like recycled water-cooling.
- Southern Nevada: Local building codes banned evaporative cooling in new developments.
- Growing calls for mandatory WUE reporting and water usage caps.

### Data Commonly Cited in Public Discussion:
- "Each ChatGPT query uses 500ml of water" (commonly cited but debated figure)
- "Training GPT-3 consumed 700,000 liters of water"
- "AI could use more water than half the UK by 2027"
- Comparisons to household water usage (1 data center = 1,000-50,000 homes)

## 5. KEY DATA POINTS & STATISTICS

### Gallons of Water per MWh for Evaporative Cooling

**On-site (direct) data center cooling:**
- WUE industry average: 1.80 L/kWh (source: Meta Platforms)
- Evaporative-heavy facilities: up to 2.5 L/kWh (~660 gallons/MWh)
- Berkeley Lab projected average WUE (beyond 2023): 0.45-0.48 L/kWh
- A 1-MW data center using traditional cooling: ~6.75 million gallons/year

**Off-site (indirect) water for electricity generation:**
- Coal plants: 19,185 gallons per MWh
- Natural gas plants: 2,800 gallons per MWh
- National average (2023): 1.2 gallons per kWh (1,200 gallons/MWh)
- 72% of total water consumption associated with data centers occurs off-site, tied to electricity generation

### Google, Microsoft, Meta Water Consumption (2023-2025)

**Google:**
- 2023: 6.1 billion gallons (23.1 billion liters) consumed by data centers (95% of total 6.4 billion gallons)
- 2024: Nearly 6 billion gallons (22.7 billion liters); 8% annual increase
- Water footprint grew 17% from 2022 to 2023
- Single largest facility: Council Bluffs, Iowa consumed 1 billion gallons (3.8 billion liters) in 2024
- 2024 water stewardship projects replenished ~4.5 billion gallons (17 billion liters), covering 64% of freshwater consumption

**Microsoft:**
- FY2023-2024: ~6.4 million cubic meters (~1.69 billion gallons) globally
- 34% increase year-over-year
- Average WUE: 0.30 L/kWh (varies: Arizona 1.52 L/kWh; Singapore 0.02 L/kWh)
- Water replenishment contracted: 50+ million cubic meters (FY24)
- New zero-water-cooling data center design launched August 2024, saving 125+ million liters/year per facility
- Acknowledged they are "not on track" for water reduction goals

**Meta:**
- 2023: 813 million gallons globally (3.1 billion liters); 95% (776 million gallons / 2.9 billion liters) from data centers
- Recently completed facilities: WUE of 0.20 L/kWh
- 2024: Water restoration projects restored over 1.6 billion gallons to high/medium water stress regions
- 2030 goal: Restore 200% of consumed water in high stress regions, 100% in medium stress regions

**AWS (Amazon):**
- WUE: 0.15 L/kWh (significantly below industry average)

**Combined Big Tech:**
- Google, Microsoft, Apple, and Meta together consumed 132 million cubic meters (~34.9 billion gallons) of water (reported 2025)

### Water Usage Per ChatGPT Query / AI Inference

**The Original Research (Li et al., 2023):**
- Paper: "Making AI Less 'Thirsty': Uncovering and Addressing the Secret Water Footprint of AI Models" (Pengfei Li et al., UC Riverside & UT Arlington)
- Published in *Communications of the ACM* (2025, peer-reviewed version); originally arXiv 2023
- GPT-3 inference: 500ml bottle of water for roughly 10-50 medium-length responses (depending on time/location)
- That translates to roughly 10-50ml per individual response
- Training GPT-3 in Microsoft's U.S. data centers: directly evaporated 700,000 liters of clean freshwater
- Total training water (including indirect): 5.4 million liters
- Source: https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.03271

**Sam Altman's Claim (June 2025):**
- Average ChatGPT query: 0.000085 gallons of water (~0.3ml), roughly "one-fifteenth of a teaspoon"
- Also stated: 0.34 watt-hours of electricity per query
- Caveats: Not peer-reviewed. Unclear what "average query" means. Excludes training water and indirect water for electricity generation. Excludes deep research requests.
- Source: https://www.datacenterdynamics.com/en/news/sam-altman-chatgpt-queries-consume-034-watt-hours-of-electricity-and-0000085-gallons-of-water/

**Academic Counter-Estimates:**
- Some researchers estimate ~1.2ml per query (4x Altman's figure) assuming a short prompt under typical conditions
- A medium-length GPT-4o query: 1.75 watt-hours x 2ml/Wh = ~3.5ml per response
- Each 100-word AI prompt: ~519ml (University of California, Riverside estimate, likely includes indirect water)
- Source: https://www.seangoedecke.com/water-impact-of-ai/

**The Discrepancy Explained:**
- Altman's figure (0.3ml): Direct on-site cooling water only
- Li et al. figure (10-50ml per response): Includes on-site + off-site electricity water
- 519ml figure: Likely includes full lifecycle (on-site + electricity generation + manufacturing)
- Infrastructure and location choices matter 10-25x more than individual query variation

Sources:
- https://oecd.ai/en/wonk/how-much-water-does-ai-consume
- https://www.ie.edu/insights/articles/from-cloud-to-cup-how-much-water-does-your-chatgpt-drink/
- https://theconversation.com/ai-has-a-hidden-water-cost-heres-how-to-calculate-yours-263252

### Projected Data Center Water Consumption by 2030

**Global Projections:**
- Current: ~560 billion liters annually
- By 2030: Could rise to ~1,200 billion liters (more than doubling)
- World Resources Institute: 1.1-1.7 trillion gallons of freshwater annually by 2030 (roughly equivalent to yearly water use of all households in California)
- Without mitigation: global water consumption could increase more than 7x by mid-century

**U.S. Projections (Lawrence Berkeley National Lab, 2024 Report):**
- 2023: 17 billion gallons (64 billion liters) direct cooling water
- 2023: 211 billion gallons (800 billion liters) indirect water (electricity generation)
- By 2028: Direct cooling water could double (34 billion gallons) or quadruple (68 billion gallons)
- U.S. data centers will require 697 million to 1.45 billion additional gallons of daily water supply by 2030

**State-Level:**
- Texas: 49 billion gallons in 2025, up to 399 billion gallons by 2030

**By 2027 (Li et al. projection):**
- Global AI demand: 4.2-6.6 billion cubic meters of water withdrawal
- Equivalent to total annual water withdrawal of 4-6 Denmarks, or half the United Kingdom

**Energy Context Driving Water Growth:**
- Data centers expected to consume up to 1,050 TWh annually by 2030
- U.S. data centers: 4.4% of national electricity consumption (up from 1.9% in 2018); could climb to 12% by 2028
- Average rack density expected to grow from 36 kW (2023) to 50 kW (2027)

## 7. CHIP MANUFACTURING WATER (BONUS)

- Ultrapure water production ratio: 1,500 gallons piped water produces 1,000 gallons ultrapure water
- Average chip fabrication facility: ~10 million gallons ultrapure water daily
- Single chip lifetime water consumption: Thousands of gallons before installation
- Approximately 80% of withdrawn water evaporates; remaining 20% discharged to municipal facilities
